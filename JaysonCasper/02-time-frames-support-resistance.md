# Time Frames, Support & Resistance

Source lesson: time-frames-support-resistance

## Timeframes
- Every timeframe shows the *same* price action, just at different resolution (zoom level) — not a different market.
- Need two roles at minimum: an **environmental/macro timeframe** (bigger picture, trend context) and an **execution timeframe** (where you actually enter). Beginners should stick to just these two and add more only with experience.
- Instructor's personal stack: daily/4H for macro environment, 4H down to 30m for local environment, 1m–12m for entries.
- Lower-timeframe signals must be interpreted *through the lens* of the higher-timeframe trend — e.g. a 1m uptrend inside a 4H downtrend is read as a retracement, not a new trend. Market Cipher B signals on a low timeframe get the same treatment: a green dot on 1m inside a 4H red-dot downtrend is a bounce/retracement signal, not a reversal call.

## Support & Resistance — core concept
- Support = price floor; resistance = price ceiling.
- **Polarity flip**: a broken support becomes the new resistance, and a broken resistance becomes the new support (analogy: floor of one story = ceiling of the story below).
- Default bias: look to **short at resistance, long at support** — fading into supports/resistances works far better than buying resistance or selling support.
- A "rejection" = price pushes into a level and fails to hold beyond it, then reverses.

## Marking levels — top-down analysis method
1. Start on the **weekly** timeframe. Scan left-to-right for "sticking points" — areas where candle bodies repeatedly open/close or wick to the same price, indicating heavy historical liquidity (lots of buy/sell activity clustered there). Large players need to return to these zones to fill big orders, which is why price reacts there again later.
2. Mark a horizontal line at each sticking point, label it by timeframe (e.g. "Weekly").
3. Repeat on **daily** (use candle bodies or wicks), then **4H**, working down through timeframes, adding more localized levels as you go lower (4H levels don't need to look far back in history; 1m levels are hyper-local).
4. Also mark all-time highs / major swing peaks — these often act as support/resistance later.
5. Rule of thumb: **higher timeframe levels are more significant** and will hold up against lower-timeframe candles; a 1-minute level won't stop a 4H candle, but a weekly/daily level will.

## Trade management example (illustrative)
- Enter short at a rejection off resistance; stop above the last swing high.
- Take partial profit (~30–40%) at the first support level reached, then move stop to breakeven ("locking in profits" — worst case becomes a scratch/win).
- Take further partials at subsequent support levels, let a runner ride.
- Take-profit levels are themselves just prior support/resistance levels on the lower (execution) timeframe.

## Trend lines & channels
- **Trend lines**: connect a series of swing highs (or swing lows) to create a diagonal support/resistance line. Subjective/art rather than exact science — the goal is finding a line price visibly respects, then trading bounces/rejections off it same as a horizontal level.
- **Channel**: price contained between two parallel trend lines (top + bottom). Non-parallel converging trend lines (like a rising/falling wedge or triangle) are a different, related pattern.
- Long/short terminology reminder: long = buy expecting price to rise; short = sell (borrow/sell first) expecting price to fall.
