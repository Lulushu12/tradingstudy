## Chart patterns — general framing
- Patterns are price-action shapes formed by connecting common price points (closes, highs, lows) over time; used to anticipate future direction. Apply to any tradable asset, not crypto-specific.
- Patterns can resolve bullish, bearish, or neutral — the instructor notes crypto patterns often break in either direction regardless of the pattern's "textbook" bias (e.g. head-and-shoulders or descending triangles breaking upward against their typical bias).

## Ascending triangle
- Structure: a flat horizontal resistance line (price rejected at the same level repeatedly) + a rising trend line connecting increasingly higher lows underneath.
- Textbook bias: bullish — each successive seller push-down fails to reach as low as the last, implying buyers are gradually winning, typically breaking out through resistance once price compresses into roughly the last ~26% of the triangle (the apex).
- Price target projection: measure the vertical height of the triangle at its widest point (flat top to the start of the rising trend line) and project that same distance upward from the breakout point.
- Practical note: these show up more reliably on lower timeframes for BTC; less common/clean on higher timeframes.

## Symmetrical triangle
- Structure: both boundary lines slope toward each other (a descending upper line and an ascending lower line), rather than one flat + one sloped.
- Same measuring-move logic as the ascending triangle: take the triangle's vertical height and project it in the direction of the eventual breakout.
- No inherent directional bias (unlike the ascending triangle) — direction is determined by which way it actually breaks, not predicted by the shape itself.
- Acknowledged explicitly: measured-move price targets from these patterns won't always be hit precisely — treat them as a general guide, not a precise target.
