# Moving Averages & Confluence

Source lesson: moving-averages-confluence
(Recorded as a live stream — Q&A chatter and personal anecdotes omitted below; only the teaching content is summarized.)

## Top-down analysis, reinforced
- Draw S/R starting on the highest timeframe you care about (weekly, or daily first), scanning from the **left** of the chart for repeated sticking points, then step down timeframe by timeframe (daily → 4H → your lowest working timeframe, e.g. 24-minute).
- **Color-code by timeframe** (e.g. weekly=yellow, daily=orange, 4H=blue/purple) and label each line — critical so you always know how much weight to give a level (a 1-minute level ≠ a weekly level).
- Higher timeframe levels = more significant reactions (bigger bounce/rejection). The more candles/time price spent forming a level, the stronger it is.
- Practical filter: don't over-draw — a level only counts if you can see price actually interacting with it repeatedly to the *left*; don't mark lines just because they'd look nice going forward. Too many lines makes the chart unreadable and less useful.
- Whether a level holds or breaks isn't decided by the level itself — that's read from momentum/volume indicators (Market Cipher, in this course).

## Moving averages — the basics
- **SMA (simple moving average)**: arithmetic mean of the last N candles.
- **EMA (exponential moving average)**: weighted average favoring recent candles — more responsive, and the instructor's preference for crypto over SMA.
- EMAs act as **dynamic support/resistance** (they move with price, unlike static horizontal S/R): price bouncing off an EMA in an uptrend, or rejecting off it in a downtrend, is a recurring pattern worth trading.
- EMA cross signals: price crossing above an EMA from below = bullish signal; crossing below = bearish signal. Also usable as a stop-loss anchor (place stop just beyond the EMA you're using as trend support).

## "Blue/Green/Yellow/Red" EMA stack strategy
- Plot 4 EMAs using Fibonacci-sequence lengths: **8, 13, 21, 55** (colored blue/green/yellow/red by convention).
- All 4 stacking in order (blue on top down to red) = long signal / uptrend confirmation. Reverse order (red on top down to blue) = short signal / downtrend confirmation.
- Price often reacts (bounces/rejects) right at one of these EMA lines, not just at the moment they cross.
- These lengths/colors are a starting template — instructor encourages testing other lengths (including non-Fibonacci ones) per asset, since some coins react better to a custom length (example given: tuning a single EMA to 76 or 83 for a specific altcoin).
- Market Cipher A ships with an EMA ribbon (multiple EMAs of undisclosed length) that changes shading when price is confirmed above/below — same "stacked EMA" concept, but the ribbon lags real-time signals a bit, so the instructor personally trades with only 1–2 of the ribbon lines exposed (e.g. ribbon 5 = 21 EMA) rather than the full ribbon.

## Core principle: confluence
- The recurring thesis of the whole course: don't trade off any single tool. Look for multiple independent things pointing to the same price/time — hand-drawn weekly/daily S&R + an EMA level/cross + a Market Cipher B momentum signal (anchor wave → trigger wave, money flow crossing red→green or green→red) all lining up together is the highest-conviction entry.
- Example pattern described repeatedly: price reclaims a major weekly resistance-turned-support, EMA confirms bullish (color flip), and Market Cipher B prints a trigger wave with money flow crossing into green — three-way confluence = strong long signal. Mirror version (rejection at resistance + bearish EMA cross + red trigger wave/money flow crossing down) = strong short signal.
- Higher timeframes give more reliable confluence signals than lower ones (more data smooths out noise).

## Practical Q&A notes worth keeping
- **Stop-loss placement**: match the EMA/timeframe to your holding horizon — a scalp uses a lower-timeframe EMA as stop reference; a multi-day swing trade uses a higher-timeframe EMA (e.g. 4H 21-EMA), which requires much wider stop room, hence why leverage should be reduced for longer-held swing trades.
- **Too many S/R lines**: yes, this is a real failure mode — only keep lines you can verify against real price history to the left; drop lines that don't have a clear historical basis.
- **Averaging into a position**: adding to a winning trade means your average entry price rises with it (position sizing example given: taking 10% profit off a position lets you redeploy more capital at the same dollar risk).
- Distinguished related-but-different indicators mentioned in Q&A: Wolfpack ID (an ADX+moving-average blend per a viewer) vs. Money Flow vs. VWAP — visually similar but not interchangeable; instructor treats Wolfpack as a supplementary filter, not a primary signal.
- Heikin Ashi candles: don't materially change where support/resistance levels are, but can visually smooth wicks — purely a personal-preference choice for level-drawing clarity, not a different price series.
