# Chickens Drinking Water (Swing Failure Pattern) — Complete Tutorial

Source lesson: chickens-drinking-water-swing-failure-pattern
(Full standalone tutorial on the SFP setup referenced throughout the rest of the course; "chickens drinking water" is the instructor's own nickname for a swing failure pattern.)

## What a swing failure pattern (SFP) actually is
- Price naturally moves in alternating swing highs/lows. A **swing failure** = price wicks *beyond* a previous swing high (or low) but the candle **closes back on the other side** of it — i.e. it fails to establish a genuine new swing high/low.
  - Bearish SFP: candle wicks above a prior swing high, closes back below it.
  - Bullish SFP: candle wicks below a prior swing low, closes back above it.
- Important distinction: if the candle actually closes beyond the prior high/low, that's a **successful** breakout, not an SFP — even if price later reverses from there, that's a different pattern ("failed auction"), not a chickens-drinking-water/SFP setup.

## How to build conviction for an SFP trade (checklist, roughly in the order given)
0. Mark the previous swing high/low that price needs to wick beyond and fail to close past.
1. **Money flow diverging from price across multiple timeframes** — e.g. price making a higher high while money flow is trending lower over that same stretch (checked on several timeframes, from the entry timeframe up to higher ones).
2. **Momentum/price divergence across multiple timeframes** — same concept as the dedicated Divergences lesson, checked at multiple timeframes for the same event.
3. **Tight/converging RSI+Stochastic RSI** on Market Cipher B — described as "the icing on the cake": when the RSI lines bunch tightly together right as the wick occurs, that's read as a strong extra signal a reversal is imminent.
4. **Price is at a meaningful level** (Fibonacci golden pocket, point of control, major S&R) when the SFP occurs — same confluence principle as the rest of the course; an SFP with no level backing it is weaker.
5. (Optional, from order flow tools like ExoCharts) **Trapped-trader imbalance**: a delta/order-flow view showing a large imbalance of buyers vs. sellers within the wick that then gets left "underwater" once the candle closes back — those trapped positions are argued to add selling/liquidation pressure that reinforces the reversal. Instructor states he doesn't personally trade off order flow/delta but explains it for viewers who do.

## Timeframe mechanics
- SFPs can form on **any timeframe** — the same 12-minute or 1-hour SFP will look far more obvious/clean on a much lower timeframe (e.g. 1-minute) zoomed into that same window, where the divergence and reversal are easier to see and time an entry off of.
- Practical workflow: identify the SFP on a higher timeframe first (where the level was originally set), then drop to a very low timeframe (1–3 minute) to find the actual divergence/RSI-tightening confirmation and get a tighter entry.
- Caution against pre-empting: don't short into the pump *before* the wick reverses ("don't grab the rising knife") — wait for price to actually come back down/up through the level before entering, unless deliberately taking on more risk for a better price ("front-running," acknowledged as something the instructor does sometimes but flagged as higher risk).
- For confirmation-seeking traders: wait for the actual reversal dot (red dot for a bearish SFP, green for bullish) to print on Market Cipher B before entering, rather than anticipating it — trades this way are described as generally winning, at the cost of a slightly worse entry price.

## Entry and risk management specifics
- Enter with a **market order**, not a resting limit order — because you can't know in advance exactly how far the wick will extend before it reverses.
- Stop loss goes beyond the wick's extreme (the swing high/low that got wicked through).
- Typical position sizing example given: risking ~1–2% of account equity per trade via stop-loss distance and leverage sizing.
- **Profit-taking discipline specific to this setup**: because SFP reversals vary a lot in strength ("sometimes the chickens are very thirsty, other times not thirsty at all"), take an initial partial (~30–50%) very early — as soon as price has moved roughly 0.5% in your favor — and immediately move the stop to breakeven. This converts every valid SFP entry into either a guaranteed small win or a bigger win, never a loss once that first partial is banked (assuming no slippage).

## Standing takeaway
This lesson formalizes a pattern referenced elsewhere in the course (e.g. Market Cipher 103's trade examples) into its own explicit, repeatable checklist: level → SFP wick+close → multi-timeframe money-flow/momentum divergence → tight RSI → (optional) order-flow imbalance → market-order entry → early partial profit to lock in a risk-free trade.
