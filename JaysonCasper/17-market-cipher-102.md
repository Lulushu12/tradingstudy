## Core idea: read Market Cipher B as one continuous structure across timeframes
The central technique of this lesson: take a drawing/brush tool and trace the overall shape of Market Cipher B's waves (connecting the tops, then separately the bottoms) rather than fixating on individual wave height or dot color. This traced outline reveals tops/bottoms of momentum even when the raw waves look noisy.

## Anchor waves and anchor regions (refined definition)
- An **anchor wave** = a momentum wave marking a clear top or bottom of momentum, giving a fixed reference point ("holds your ship in place") that all subsequent waves are judged against.
- When no single wave is clean enough to call an anchor, use an **anchor region** instead — a general zone where the traced outline is topping/bottoming, even without a single standout wave.
- Three factors that make a candidate anchor wave more reliable:
  1. It extends past ±60 (deeper = more reliable).
  2. It coincides with a money-flow color change (red→green or green→red) at the same point.
  3. A clear top/bottom is visible in the traced outline independent of the wave itself.

## Trigger waves (refined definition)
- A trigger wave shows **clear directional progress relative to the anchor** — literally just "is this wave further along/higher than the last, in the direction we expect." Perfect textbook trigger sequences (clean higher-lows or lower-highs you could draw a straight line through) are rare and are the strongest version of the signal; looser, still-directionally-consistent sequences still count, just with less confidence.
- Direction is always judged relative to the established anchor, not in isolation.

## Multi-timeframe technique (the lesson's main practical contribution)
Two directions of analysis depending on what you're actually trying to trade:
- **Top-down (start on a high timeframe, work down)** — used when you want to trade a **lower** timeframe. Trace the outline on a higher timeframe (e.g. 4H) first to establish the macro anchor/direction, then drop down through 3H → 2H → 24-min → 12-min etc. Once the higher-timeframe anchor region is established, every money-flow-cross + trigger-wave setup on the lower timeframes becomes an actionable entry *without* needing to wait for the higher timeframe's own dot to print — because you already know its general direction from the trace.
- **Bottom-up (start on a low timeframe, work up)** — used when you want to trade a **higher** timeframe. Trace the outline starting on a very low timeframe (e.g. 5-min), where a bottom/top confirms much earlier than it would on higher timeframes. Once confirmed on the low timeframe, that bottom/top usually "previews" what the higher timeframe (e.g. 2H, 3H) will eventually confirm — letting you enter the higher-timeframe trade well before its own signal prints. Recommended step size: don't jump too many timeframe levels at once (e.g. 5-min → 20-min → 1-hour, not 5-min straight to 3-hour) since intermediate steps keep the read cleaner.
- Explicit caveat from Q&A: you must still **wait for the lower timeframe to actually confirm its own bottom/top** (via a secondary reference wave establishing clear direction change) before treating it as reliable — you cannot call a bottom in real time before it's actually formed. The advantage is that this confirmation still comes far earlier than the higher timeframe's own confirmation would.
- 12-minute is given as the practical lower bound for this technique when trading off a 4H-anchored structure — below that, money-flow crosses happen too fast/noisily to reliably judge in real time; 24-minute is described as cleaner/more reliable than 12-minute for this reason.

## Trade management for this "free-flow" (non-checklist) style
Since this approach relies on reading structure rather than a fixed confirmation checklist, risk management is emphasized more heavily than usual:
- Always place the stop **below the invalidating swing low** (for longs) — if that low breaks, the "we're in an uptrend" thesis is wrong, exit.
- As the trade progresses and new higher lows print, trail the stop up beneath each new higher low — this guarantees the trade can't turn into a loser once enough progress has been banked.
- Take partial profit at prior resistance/support levels identified the normal way (hand-drawn S&R), same staged-TP approach as the rest of the course.
- Judge entries by risk/reward, not just direction: an entry where the required stop distance is much larger than the distance to the next resistance/take-profit is a poor risk/reward trade and should be skipped even if directionally correct.

## Divergence vs. momentum — explicit distinction
- **Momentum** = oscillator and price moving in the *same* direction (e.g. both rising) — this is confirmation, not a divergence.
- **Divergence** = oscillator and price moving in *opposite* directions from a common reference point (draw a trend line on price and a matching one on the oscillator's peaks/troughs from the same starting point) — a warning of an impending reversal.
  - Bearish divergence: price makes higher highs, oscillator makes lower highs → price is "overvalued" relative to the momentum backing it, correction expected.
  - Bullish divergence: price makes lower lows, oscillator makes higher lows → price is "undervalued," upward correction expected.
- Reliability: described as playing out "9 out of 10 times," though the instructor notes higher-timeframe divergences (daily/weekly) are rarer and less historically tested, so should be taken seriously but treated as somewhat uncharted.
- Divergences aren't necessarily instant — the instructor also flags that on rare occasions a divergence takes a while to resolve, or resolves only partially before the trend reasserts, so "still expect it to play out, but be cautious" if it doesn't resolve immediately.

## Repeating patterns
- Watch for a Market Cipher B "shape" (anchor → trigger sequence → money-flow cross → strong move) that has already played out 2+ times historically on the same asset/timeframe — treat a recurrence of that same shape as a higher-confidence setup, especially for swing entries. Two BTC 4H historical examples were walked through where the same anchor/trigger/money-flow-cross shape preceded a strong move each time it appeared.

## Q&A points worth keeping
- **Trading with money flow "against" you**: it's fine to short on a low timeframe while money flow is still green on a higher timeframe (or long while red), but only with strong additional confirmation (e.g. a secondary indicator confirming early) — otherwise it's easy to get chopped up by a red/green wave that turns out to be small rather than a real reversal.
- A viewer highlighted using a supplementary indicator (**Wolfpack ID**, a free TradingView indicator) as an early cross signal that can precede/confirm a Market Cipher money-flow bottom even while money flow is still in the "wrong" color — instructor treats this as a useful extra confluence layer worth exploring, not a primary tool.
- Reiterated from the S&R lessons: always mark levels by scanning **left** on the chart (historical liquidity zones), because price tends to revert to areas where a lot of past buying/selling happened — and those hand-drawn zones frequently coincide with Fibonacci levels pulled independently, reinforcing that "starting from the left" is grounded in real liquidity, not just a habit.
