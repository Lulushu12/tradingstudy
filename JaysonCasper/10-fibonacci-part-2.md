# Fibonacci Part 2

Source lesson: fibonacci-part-2
(Reinforces Part 1's retracement/extension mechanics with more detail on confirming bounces and structuring entries/stops.)

## Recap of mechanics (same as Part 1)
- Uptrend → pull retracement swing low to swing high, look for reaction at 0.382 / 0.5 / 0.618 / 0.786.
- Downtrend → pull swing high to swing low; same levels, but reactions are rejections rather than bounces.
- Always wick-to-wick or body-to-body, never mixed.

## New: how to judge whether a Fib level will hold (not just where it is)
- On a lower timeframe (e.g. 1–3 min) at the moment price taps a Fib level, look for:
  - A **bullish/bearish divergence** between price and Market Cipher momentum (price making a lower low while momentum makes a higher low = bullish divergence, and vice versa).
  - A **money-flow crossover** starting to turn (described as: the losing-color flow band thinning out and the new color starting to poke through) right at the level.
- On the 4H timeframe specifically: **two consecutive deep momentum waves below -60 on Market Cipher B** at a Fib level is called (informally) a strong signal the retracement has bottomed — described as correct roughly 9 out of 10 times in the examples shown.

## Target-setting method (elaborated)
1. Confirm retracement is over (via the Market Cipher signals above).
2. Pull a **second** Fib retracement from the original swing high down to the exact retracement low.
3. Rule of thumb given a specific confidence figure: **the first take-profit target lands between the 0.382–0.5 level of that second fib "the vast majority of the time"** (instructor states figures in the 85–99% range across different examples — treat as an approximate, not literal, statistic).
4. Continue watching subsequent fib levels (0.618, 0.786, 1.0, 1.272, 1.618...) as further resistance/support/take-profit rungs — repeatedly shown holding almost to-the-cent in the BTC/ETH/LTC examples given.

## Trend-Based Fib Extension (elaborated)
- Needs 3 clicks: swing low → swing high → second swing low (for an uptrend continuation), or the mirrored 3 points for a downtrend.
- Produces an extension ladder (0.382, 0.5, 0.618, 0.786, 1.0/"100%", 1.272, 1.618, etc.) projecting where price may go on the next leg — shown holding as resistance/support repeatedly on ETH and LTC examples.
- Bull-market observation: each successive uptrend impulse wave has tended to be larger than the previous one (illustrated on Bitcoin's full history) — used qualitatively to argue for continuation targets in a strong trend, not as a strict rule.

## Using Fib levels to structure an actual trade (entry / stop / target)
- The point of pulling these levels isn't just prediction — it defines **where to put your stop loss** so risk is capped: enter on the confirmed bounce off a Fib support, place the stop just below that support/swing low, and target the next Fib extension levels above.
- Example given: risking ~2% (stop below the support) to target ~19%+ (next extension levels) if the bounce plays out — the reason this is safer than an arbitrary entry is that the invalidation point (no bounce = plan is wrong) is clearly defined by the level itself.

## Triple/quadruple confluence (main takeaway, restated)
- Never trade a Fibonacci level alone. Stack it with: hand-drawn daily/weekly S&R, EMAs (e.g. 21/55 EMA sitting right at the same price), and volume nodes (VPVR high-volume node at the same price) — when 3+ of these coincide at one zone, that's treated as an unusually strong support/resistance area worth waiting for/trading off of.
- Market Cipher (bottom/top formation, money flow crossing, VWAP crossing zero) is used as the final trigger/confirmation once price is sitting at one of these high-confluence zones — not as a lone signal by itself.
