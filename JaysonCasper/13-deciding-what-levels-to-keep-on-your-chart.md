# Deciding What Levels to Keep on Your Chart

Source lesson: deciding-what-levels-to-keep-on-your-chart

## The core problem this lesson addresses
Charts get cluttered with every S&R line, Fib level, and volume node ever drawn, to the point of being unusable. The fix isn't to draw fewer levels initially — it's to filter aggressively down to only the levels with **confluence**.

## 3-step process ("do your TA → check your indicators → execute")
1. **Do the technical analysis** at whatever scope matches your trading style: scalpers/day traders do it each morning on lower timeframes (e.g. 12-min, looking back ~7 days); swing traders do it roughly weekly on higher timeframes (daily). Mark levels, but specifically look for places where **multiple independent tools land on the same price** — that's a confluence zone worth marking.
2. **Check indicators (Market Cipher) for confirmation** once price actually returns to a marked confluence zone — set a price alert at the level rather than watching constantly; when the alert fires, look for a divergence forming on a lower timeframe (mentions 24-minute and 6-minute as the ones checked in the examples).
3. **Execute** only once both (1) a genuine confluence zone and (2) a live divergence/confirmation are present — this two-condition requirement is the actual filter for which trades get taken.

## Practical chart-hygiene tip
Once a confluence zone is confirmed as significant, delete the individual Fib lines/volume markers that produced it and replace them with a single plain line in a dedicated "key levels" layer/folder. Toggle that folder on/off instead of leaving every raw analysis tool visible — keeps the working chart clean while still preserving the reasoning if needed.

## Worked examples referenced
- A long entry nicknamed the "6 POC golden nugget" setup: a fixed-range point of control landed exactly on a 0.618 Fibonacci retracement level (two-way confluence). Entry trigger was a bullish divergence on the 6-minute chart (the "uneven [momentum wave]" pattern referenced in earlier lessons) as price dipped into that exact level.
- A short entry: a value-area-high (from a fixed volume range) coincided with a 0.786 Fibonacci level (and reportedly also a previous-day point of control) — three-way confluence. Trigger was a bearish divergence on the 24-minute chart as price spiked into the zone.
- A subsequent long entry at a golden pocket, illustrating that the same divergence-on-24-minute confirmation applies symmetrically for both long and short setups once a real confluence zone has been identified.
- Common thread across both real trades cited: the momentum divergence that triggered entry consistently showed up specifically on the **24-minute chart**, even though the underlying support/resistance analysis was done on other timeframes — used as the instructor's personal go-to confirmation timeframe.

## Standing takeaway
Confluence (multiple independent methods agreeing on one price) is what separates a "level worth watching" from clutter; the indicator confirmation (Market Cipher divergence) is what separates "watching" from "entering."
