# Market Cipher 103 — Part 2

Source lesson: market-cipher-103-part-2
(Continuation of Part 1's 6-timeframe framework, walked through with two more real trade examples — a short and two longs.)

## Same process, applied to messier/less obvious setups
Part 1 used an unusually clean example; this lesson deliberately picks less obvious cases to show how to weigh mixed signals rather than expecting every timeframe to agree perfectly.

## Key refinement: environmental timeframes can be "neutral," not just bull/bear
- On the 4H chart for the short example, 2 of the 3 Cipher B signals (momentum, money flow) were bullish and only the VWAP was bearish — labeled **neutral**, not automatically bearish just because price was at resistance. Only once the 2H and 1H also leaned bearish (via an about-to-print red dot, i.e. a momentum wave clearly topping, even without a full divergence) did the overall environmental bias become bearish.
- Explicit rule stated: **a momentum wave topping out (about to print a red dot) on a higher timeframe (4H/2H/1H) is itself treated as a bearish sign**, even with no formal divergence — "every red dot on the 1-hour, we always come down at least a little" was given as a reliable minor effect, useful even for small scalps.

## Hidden divergence introduced (distinct from regular divergence)
- **Regular divergence**: two adjacent momentum waves, one up while price is down (or vice versa) — a reversal signal (as covered in Market Cipher 102).
- **Hidden divergence**: comparing two momentum waves that are *not* adjacent — e.g. in an established trend, a low followed later by a higher low on the momentum oscillator even while price prints a lower low — read the same way, just harder to spot since the waves aren't next to each other. Both are described as equally valid "don't get hung up on which type it is."

## Reading momentum waves that aren't clean "humps"
- Clarified that a momentum wave doesn't need to visually look like a smooth hump — any two wave-crossover points (marked by dots) can be compared for divergence purposes, even if the shape between them is irregular. The comparison that matters is wave-height trend over time, not visual shape.

## Long trade #1 (daily support bounce, ~8.6% move)
- Entered on a bounce off a daily support level. Before entering: 4H showed an early (not-yet-confirmed) bullish divergence between price and momentum; 2H was closer to neutral (about to print a green dot); 1H showed a slight but real bullish divergence in both momentum and money flow → environmental bias: bullish.
- Lower timeframes (24-min mostly neutral/unclear, 12-min showing an emerging bullish divergence, 3-min and especially 1-min showing very clear divergence across momentum, money flow, and VWAP simultaneously) confirmed the entry.
- Entry could be taken either aggressively (right at the reaction wick) or conservatively (after price prints a confirmed higher low) — instructor frames this as a risk/reward tradeoff: earlier entry = more risk and more reward; later, confirmed entry = less of both. Example given: an aggressive entry captured ~8%, a late entry still captured ~5%.

## Long trade #2 (bounce off a prior swing low used as new support, deep drawdown context)
- Context: price crashing toward a widely-watched major support; instructor specifically calls out that **previous significant lows (even old ones, like a prior all-time low) can act as support on the way down**, and that a wick below such a level followed by a reclaim is a bounce pattern worth watching for.
- 4H showed a large, unmistakable bullish divergence (price down ~17% while momentum printed higher lows) — labeled "super bullish." 2H and 1H reinforced it (including a hidden divergence on the 1H).
- Lower-timeframe walkthrough (24-min → 12-min → 3-min → 1-min) repeats the now-familiar pattern: keep drilling down until money flow, VWAP, and momentum waves all show divergence together right at the level, then look for a physical reaction (a sharp wick reversal, e.g. ~3% move in 2 minutes in this example) as the actual trigger.
- Important nuance flagged: an *earlier* touch of a similar-looking support box did **not** get taken as an entry because Market Cipher wasn't showing divergence there yet — reinforcing that price merely being at "a level" isn't sufficient; the Cipher B confirmation must also be present at that specific touch, not just at the general zone.
- Risk-acknowledgment: if you take an aggressive reaction-based entry and get stopped out (a small loss, e.g. ~1%) before the real bottom, that's an accepted cost of the method ("catching a falling knife") — the instructor's stance is that gains from correctly-confirmed entries elsewhere will outweigh these occasional small losses, provided position sizing/risk management (covered in a later lesson) is followed.

## Standing takeaway
Same core process as Part 1 (level → environmental bias → execution-timeframe confirmation → physical reaction), demonstrated on messier real trades to show how to average partially-conflicting signals into one directional bias rather than requiring unanimous agreement across every timeframe and every Cipher B component.
