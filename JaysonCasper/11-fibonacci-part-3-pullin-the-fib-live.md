## How the instructor actually decides where to pull a Fib from
- **Finding support in an uptrend**: the swing high used stays fixed (the top of the current leg); pull *multiple* Fib retracements from different, progressively more recent swing lows up to that same swing high as price keeps making new local lows on the way down. Each pull gives a fresh set of possible support levels (favorites: 0.382 and 0.618).
- **Finding resistance in a downtrend**: mirrored — the swing low stays fixed, pull from different swing highs down to that same low.
- A "swing low/high" for this purpose is loose: any local pullback bottom (or peak) counts, not just the single most extreme point — so several valid Fib pulls can coexist for the same trend leg.
- **Key trick for extra confluence**: when two different Fib pulls (from different swing points) put their retracement levels at the *same* price — e.g. one pull's 0.618 lands exactly on another pull's 0.382 — that overlap is treated as a much stronger level than either alone.
- Combine with other confluence as usual: daily/weekly S&R, VPVR high-volume nodes, naked POCs.

## Divergence types (elaborated beyond earlier lessons)
- **Regular divergence** = reversal signal: price and Market Cipher B momentum move in opposite directions (e.g. price makes a lower low, momentum makes a higher low → bullish reversal warning).
- **Hidden divergence** = continuation signal: price makes a higher low while momentum makes a lower low (in an uptrend) — signals the larger trend is likely to resume, not reverse.
- Strongest setups combine both across timeframes: e.g. a *hidden* divergence on a higher timeframe (confirming the macro uptrend is intact) coinciding with a *regular* divergence on a lower timeframe (signaling the end of a local pullback) = high-conviction long.
- Real trade example: hidden bullish divergence on the 2H (macro uptrend continuation) + regular bullish divergence on the 1H, entered after price dropped further into the Fibonacci "golden pocket" (0.618–0.65 zone) and a naked point of control, confirmed by a bullish divergence appearing down to the 6-minute chart before entry.

## Reading multi-timeframe momentum for trend reversals ("order of operations")
1. Price reaches a significant level (resistance for a top, support for a bottom).
2. The next-higher timeframe (e.g. 1H) starts showing its momentum wave topping/bottoming out.
3. The timeframe above that (e.g. 4H) also needs to be turning — not just the lower ones — before treating it as a real swing-level reversal rather than a scalp-only bounce.
4. Only look for swing (not scalp) reversal trades once the higher timeframes are genuinely turning; lower-timeframe (3–12 min) divergences alone are typically good for scalps only, and higher timeframes always take priority over lower ones when they conflict.

## Trade management notes mentioned
- Order laddering: split intended position size into thirds (or similar), place limit orders across a support zone rather than one price — accepts that sometimes only part of the intended size fills, in which case you either add at market or accept the smaller position.
- Habit of locking in min profit early (moving stop to breakeven after an initial partial take), then scaling out further in chunks as the trade extends — consistent with the position-management style described in earlier lessons.
- Watches for a "failed breakout" structure: if price reclaims a broken range and then falls back below the prior range's lower boundary, that invalidates the bullish continuation thesis and points to a much larger downside target instead of a healthy pullback.
